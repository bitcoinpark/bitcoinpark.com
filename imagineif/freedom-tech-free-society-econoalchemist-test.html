"It's not a matter of don't tread on me. It's that you can't tread on me. That's the point."

Econoalchemist · Imagine IF 2025 · Presentation

Econoalchemist opened his Imagine IF presentation with a line that reframed the entire conversation about digital rights. Not a request. Not a protest sign. A mathematical fact. The tools exist. The encryption works. And no government on earth can change that.

What followed was 16 minutes of controlled fire. A case for why open-source developers are the most important people in the room, and why the free society you enjoy right now depends on them continuing to ship code.

"The laws of math are stronger than the laws of man. All the violence in the world doesn't change the laws of math."

This was the thesis. Everything else in the talk built on this single premise. Encryption doesn't care about jurisdiction. A properly generated private key doesn't bend to a subpoena. The math just works.

"Without open-source developers, there is no freedom tech. And without freedom tech, there is no free society."

He said this more than once, and it deserved repeating. The tools that protect your speech, your transactions, your property, your privacy? Someone built them for free. Nostr. GrapheneOS. Signal. Start9. Sparrow. Every one of them exists because a developer chose to write code that no company commissioned and no government approved.

"Combating misinformation is just code speak for attacking free speech, suppression of dissenting opinions, conditional access, and retaliation for speaking freely."

He walked through the receipts. The EU's digital identification mandate. Google requiring government ID from Play Store developers. Twitter forcing identification in certain countries. Each one wrapped in the language of safety. Each one a new gate between you and the open internet.

"Bitcoin is not confiscatable if it's done right. And in its most simple form, Bitcoin is software. Software is code. Code is speech."

The Canadian trucker convoy was the case study. Governments froze bank accounts. They went after donors. They used the financial system as a weapon against people who disagreed with them. Bitcoin sidesteps that entirely. Ashigaru on mobile. Sparrow on desktop. Ronin and Dojo to connect to the network. CoinJoin for privacy on a public blockchain. The stack exists. Right now.

"We don't go after gun manufacturers when some psychopath comes out of a crowd and shoots Charlie Kirk in the neck. Why are we going after software developers?"

The Samourai Wallet case hit hardest. Two privacy developers facing $200 million in fines and 25 years in prison. Their crime: they wrote code that enabled anonymity on a public blockchain. Non-custodial. Never touched anyone's money. Never had custody or control. The state kicked in their doors anyway.

"The question's not who's going to let us, but who's going to stop us."

He closed with a photo of himself and his son on a roof, installing a Blockstream satellite dish to download the Bitcoin blockchain without the internet. His wife took the picture. A father teaching his kid that sovereignty isn't something you're granted. It's something you build. One open-source project at a time.
