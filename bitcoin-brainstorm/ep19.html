Bitcoin Brainstorm: Episode 19

Series Bitcoin Brainstorm
Episode 19
Format Panel Discussion
Speakers Cathie Wood (ARK Invest), Tuur Demeester (Adamant Capital), Alex Gladstein (HRF), Alex Gleason (Soapbox), Rapha Zagury (Elektron Energy), Lorenzo Valente (ARK Invest), Rod Roudi (Bitcoin Park)
Since inception, ARK has researched and published thoughts on the bitcoin ecosystem, with Big Ideas, articles, whitepapers, monthly Bitcoin reports and podcasts. In this episode of Bitcoin Brainstorm, Cathie Wood and guests reflect on ARK's decade-long Bitcoin journey, from early research to its prominent role in Big Ideas 2026, and debate Bitcoin's evolving thesis amid shifting macro conditions. The conversation distinguishes Bitcoin as "freedom technology" versus stablecoins as more "humanitarian" but censorable tools, while exploring institutional adoption, emerging-market use cases, and gold's relationship to Bitcoin as a hedge. The group explores the convergence of Bitcoin, AI, and decentralized social (Nostr), highlighting innovations in "vibe coding," open-source acceleration, and the rise of autonomous agents.

The Top and the Coming Rotation into Bitcoin

A cross-disciplinary panel argues gold's historic peak signals an imminent capital rotation into Bitcoin and a broader ecosystem of open-source tools.

At a moment when gold trades near historic highs relative to the global money supply, a panel of investors and technologists argued that capital will soon rotate from the ancient metal into Bitcoin and a broader ecosystem of open-source tools. The claim, discussed during Episode 19 of the ARK Invest Bitcoin Brainstorm, rests on macroeconomic data, but not everyone agrees the timing is clear.

ARK Invest CEO Cathie Wood pointed to gold's ratio against M2, a broad measure of money circulating in the economy. "If you look at gold relative to M2, we're at a 1.56 and there's been only one time we've been higher," she said. "That was in the Great Depression when we got to 1.71." Wood said she would sell gold now and buy Bitcoin in its place, framing the trade as a response to gold entering what she called rarified territory.

Adamant Capital founder Tuur Demeester offered a complementary but distinct reading. He described gold as "a canary in the coal mine for Bitcoin," suggesting that gold's surge signals a broader appetite for hard assets that Bitcoin will eventually absorb. Demeester also declared the four-year Bitcoin price cycle finished, predicting a structural shift in how capital flows between the two assets.

The panel extended its analysis beyond price. Alex Gladstein, chief strategy officer at the Human Rights Foundation, drew a sharp line between Bitcoin and dollar-pegged stablecoins, digital tokens designed to track the U.S. dollar's value. "Bitcoin is freedom technology," he said. "You could say stablecoins are humanitarian technology, but they're not freedom technology." The distinction matters for populations living under authoritarian monetary controls, where permissionless transactions carry different weight than access to a digital dollar.

Gladstein also flagged what he called a new renaissance in open-source software, driven by advances in artificial intelligence. He argued that AI coding tools now place independent developers on the same level as large technology firms. Wood agreed, predicting an entrepreneurial explosion as barriers to starting software companies collapse.

Open-source developer Alex Gleason demonstrated that convergence in practice. He described AI agents that manage their own Bitcoin wallets, a development that merges machine autonomy with programmable money. "Nobody is losing their jobs," Gleason said. "We are changing and adapting to a new way of doing things." The statement reflects a broader consensus on the panel that AI will reshape rather than replace existing roles.

An underlying energy discussion was woven into the conversation via Rapha Zagury, founder of Elektron Energy. His firm deploys Bitcoin mining hardware at renewable energy sites in Brazil. He described the present moment as "a crossroad" where distributed power grids and a monetary transition converge, each reinforcing the other.

Wood sketched a five-to-ten-year horizon anchored by autonomous vehicles, early-stage disease diagnosis through AI, and digital property rights. Those forecasts remain speculative, and the gold-to-Bitcoin rotation depends on macro conditions no single investor controls. Still, the breadth of agreement across finance, human rights, energy, and software development suggests a thesis worth tracking, even if its timeline stays uncertain.
