Physicist Turned Dissident Sees Freedom Tech as China's Circumvention Lifeline

The equations Xiao Qiang once studied—astrophysics, the mechanics of distant stars—could not prepare him for what he witnessed in Beijing in the spring of 1989.

The Tiananmen Square massacre ended his career as a scientist and began a different kind of life's work. Now a research scientist at UC Berkeley and founder of the China Digital Times, Qiang argues that freedom tech payment infrastructure has become the missing link between Chinese citizens and the uncensored information they are seeking.

Speaking at a fireside chat in Austin hosted by the Austin Bitcoin Club and Human Rights Foundation, Qiang traced his transformation with the precision of someone who has told the story many times but not yet made peace with it. "I became a full-time human rights activist since 1989," he recalled. "At Tiananmen Square, I went back to Beijing and ever since, my life working for human rights." That pivot from astrophysics to activism now spans more than three decades of research into digital surveillance, censorship circumvention, and what he describes as the global export of authoritarian technology.

Qiang's framing of China's AI trajectory drew on an unlikely metaphor. What began in 2003 as genuine optimism about the internet's liberating potential—"the New Hope," he said, borrowing from Star Wars—became, under Xi Jinping's consolidation of power, something closer to its opposite. He summarized the current moment without hesitation: "The Emperor Got AI." China's surveillance architecture, he explained, now integrates facial recognition, the Great Firewall—a filtering and monitoring system that tracks registered users' browsing activity—and WeChat, a super-platform covering payments, employment, and social networking, all under effective government access. Even Apple, he noted, surrenders data to operate within Chinese borders.

The reach extends beyond China's borders. Chinese surveillance technology, including facial recognition systems through smart city contracts, has spread across Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa. In societies with limited democratic accountability, Qiang said, those tools are being used against dissidents, journalists, and civil organizations. The Human Rights Foundation, which classifies China as a fully authoritarian regime on its Tyranny Tracker, frames this export as one of the most pressing threats to global freedoms.

"Only, I think, the [freedom technology] world can develop technologies to provide alternative ways to empower those otherwise sort of platforms cannot be accessible by the Chinese users. That's the task I like for all of you."

But Qiang's sharpest argument in Austin concerned a gap that technologists in the room were positioned to address. Strong demand exists inside China, he said, for access to Western large language models—AI systems not trained under state censorship. The obstacle is not technical awareness. It is payment. Chinese users relying on WeChat Pay are immediately visible to internet police.

"Only, I think, the [freedom technology] world can develop technologies to provide alternative ways to empower those otherwise sort of platforms cannot be accessible by the Chinese users," Qiang said, addressing an audience he acknowledged was already oriented toward freedom technology. "That's the task I like for all of you."

For the physicist who redirected his life toward human rights the morning after Tiananmen, the path from celestial mechanics to freedom technology advocacy follows its own kind of logic—the logic of a person who has spent 35 years looking for tools capable of reaching people that governments are determined to keep unreachable. His email, he offered at the close, is public.
