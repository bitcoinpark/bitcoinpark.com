Vibe Coding for Freedom: Building Bitcoin Into AI

Experience Austin Bitcoin Club
Date March 2026
Session Type Panel Discussion
Location Bitcoin Park Austin
Speakers Justin Moon (HRF), Mark Suman (Maple AI), Lyle Pratt (VIdA.io)
Former Lightning startup founders Mark Suman and Lyle Pratt discuss how their failures in Bitcoin payments led them to build AI applications with Bitcoin and freedom tech principles at their core, moderated by Justin Moon of the Human Rights Foundation's AI for Individual Rights program.

Two Failed Lightning Startups Later, These Founders Are Building Bitcoin Into AI

Mark Suman joined Mutiny to scale it. Within weeks, he helped shut it down instead. Lyle Pratt spent years searching for product-market fit in Lightning payments, accidentally built an AI phone agent while doing it, and never looked back.

Both founders arrived at a panel in Austin hosted by the Austin Bitcoin Club and Human Rights Foundation, carrying the same conviction they'd brought to Bitcoin—and a new set of tools to act on it.

The moderator, Justin Moon, leader of the AI for Individual Rights grant program at the Human Rights Foundation, opened with what he called a cheap shot: asking two former Lightning builders for a status update on their Lightning companies. Suman was direct. "It was a tough decision, but it was a tough space to operate in," he said of Mutiny's shutdown, acknowledging the unresolved difficulty in a room where some of those co-founders were seated. The repository sits on GitHub, unmaintained. Pratt's company followed a similar arc—years of friction, then an accidental pivot that clarified everything.

What neither of them abandoned was the underlying philosophy. Suman built Maple AI on a concept he borrowed directly from Bitcoin self-custody: every user gets their own private encryption key, generated on the client side, inaccessible to the company. "It's like you entered in one of these rooms right here and you close the door and it's just you and this open source LLM sitting there together talking," he explained. Maple accepts payment only in Bitcoin, requires no identifying information, and has been banned from the Chinese app store—a detail Suman noted without apparent regret. Users with a VPN can still reach it.

Pratt's VIdA.io operates differently but from adjacent values. The platform lets companies deploy AI agents—what he describes as software capable of running on a full browser, full file system, and full workspace, not a chat dialogue—into their own products. A concrete example: smartmoving.com, described as the largest business management software for moving companies, uses VIdA to power its integrated AI agent. Pratt's assessment of where the technology stands was unambiguous. "The singularity is here," he said, pointing to AI models that advanced from version 5.2 to 5.3 to 5.4 within months, each iteration substantially more capable than the last.

"People playing Pokemon Go with their kids didn't know that they were training army robots of the future."

Moon pressed both founders on a question that hung over the room: does the rise of AI help or hurt the project that brought most of them to Bitcoin in the first place? Suman's answer came through a story about Pokemon Go. Players who photographed urban environments a decade ago, he noted, are now unwittingly contributing to a spatial mapping project that defense contractors are using to train autonomous robots. "People playing Pokemon Go with their kids didn't know that they were training army robots of the future," he said. The lesson he drew for AI was identical: data handed to opaque systems does not stay contained. Pratt added that Nostr—a decentralized protocol—has emerged as a possible trust layer for verifying which AI agents online are authentic and which are spoofs, a problem that centralized platforms cannot currently solve.

The practical stakes, both agreed, are still ahead of most users. Suman argued that the $200-per-month compute subsidies that major AI providers are currently offering will eventually end, forcing a reckoning with true costs—and a moment when Bitcoin's microtransaction infrastructure either exists or doesn't. "We need to make sure that we have all of the infrastructure in place so that Bitcoin can be a viable option against these traditional payment systems," he said.

For two founders who once tried to build that infrastructure directly, the path curved but did not reverse. The values moved into a different vessel. The same question—who controls the money, and who controls the data—followed them into a new industry.
