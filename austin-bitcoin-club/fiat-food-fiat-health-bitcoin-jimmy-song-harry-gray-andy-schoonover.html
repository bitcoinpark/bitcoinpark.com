Bitcoin Users Turn to Direct Payment Models as Food and Healthcare Costs Soar

A growing movement of bitcoin advocates is applying "Texas hedge" principles to address America's broken food and healthcare systems through direct payment models that bypass traditional intermediaries.

At a recent Austin Bitcoin Club meetup, Jimmy Song, Harry Gray, and Andy Schoonover outlined how fiat currency's expansion through debt has corrupted both healthcare and food systems, creating monopolistic pricing that hurts consumers. Their solution: vote with your wallet by supporting direct payment alternatives.

"Something like 250,000 families every year go bankrupt due to a medical event, even though they have health insurance," said Schoonover, founder and CEO of CrowdHealth, a direct payment healthcare alternative. His company has processed over 35,000 bills, consistently achieving 50% discounts compared to traditional insurance rates by enabling patients to pay providers directly in cash.

The healthcare economics are stark. Administrative costs consume 30-40% of healthcare spending, with doctors spending 16 hours weekly fighting insurance companies for payment. Meanwhile, the Affordable Care Act's profit limitations created perverse incentives where insurance companies benefit from premium increases rather than cost controls.

"If the buyer and the seller both want the price to go up, the price is going up."

Schoonover explained, noting that health insurance companies built models to approve or deny claims based on market conditions rather than medical necessity.

The food system reveals similar patterns of fiat-driven consolidation. Gray, co-host of the Meat Mafia podcast and co-founder of Noble Origins, detailed how agricultural debt traps farmers in industrial models dependent on pesticides and subsidies.

"The education system for agriculture is entirely predicated on borrowing massive amounts of money for land, equipment, and chemicals," Gray said. "It's truly a form of slavery to the banks."

The centralization accelerated after the U.S. abandoned the gold standard in the early 1970s. Diabetes rates exploded from 1% to 10%, with another 40% of Americans now pre-diabetic. Today, 25 cents of every healthcare dollar goes toward diabetes treatment.

"Joel Salatin told us that Americans used to spend double on food but half on healthcare compared to now," Gray noted. "We've reversed it because of crappier food."

Jimmy Song, a Bitcoin developer and author of "Fiat Ruins Everything," emphasized the common thread: fiat monetary expansion through loans, which enables companies to scale into oligopolies with monopolistic pricing power that eliminates consumer choice.

The decentralization of media over the past decade has helped pierce this veil of institutional capture. Internet bloggers and independent researchers challenged the food pyramid and cholesterol myths that served corporate interests rather than public health.

Now, Bitcoin advocates see the protocol as the logical next step. CrowdHealth is in discussions with a top-10 hospital system about accepting Bitcoin payments, potentially creating circularity in healthcare's $5.6 trillion market.

"If we could get Bitcoiners on one side of the healthcare transaction and doctors on the other, transacting in Bitcoin with pennies in transaction fees happening instantaneously, that goes a long way."

Their advice is practical: support local farmers and ranchers, even at premium prices, and consider direct payment alternatives to traditional insurance. One family saves $1,000 monthly with CrowdHealth versus conventional insurance‚Äîmoney they redirect toward quality food from local sources.

"We need to vote with our wallets," Schoonover urged. "Why continue purchasing crap food and crap health insurance when we know they're leeches on society?"
