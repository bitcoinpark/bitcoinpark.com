"Is it going to pay off? We're at the five-year stage."

Joe Kelly (Unchained), Rob Hamilton (Anchorwatch), Clark Moody (Epoch VC) · Moderated by Michael Goldstein (Satoshi Nakamoto Institute) · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Panel

The first Bitcoin-only venture funds launched around 2021. Their limited partners will soon start asking for returns. Whether those returns materialize will determine the future of Bitcoin startup funding.

That was the undertone of a panel at Bitcoin Takeover with Joe Kelly of Unchained, Rob Hamilton of Anchorwatch, and Clark Moody of Epoch VC, moderated by Michael Goldstein of the Satoshi Nakamoto Institute. Goldstein opened with the question every Bitcoin founder has to answer: why not just stack sats?

"Risk and yield are two sides of the same coin. You have to drop some humility and say, no, actually I can try and build something greater."

Rob Hamilton's Bitcoin custody and insurance company, Anchorwatch, turned four this month during a period when Bitcoin's price rose only 20%. He argued the returns already justify the bet on a risk-adjusted basis, but acknowledged the leap of faith required.

Why not just hold Bitcoin? Because building creates more value than passive ownership — if you're willing to take the risk.

"There's something proven there that shows what can be at the other end of that J curve."

Joe Kelly framed Unchained's trajectory as proof of concept. The company, which offers Bitcoin financial services built on collaborative custody where clients hold their own keys, has secured over $10 billion in Bitcoin across 10,000 clients and more than 20,000 keys.

Venture returns follow a J curve — early losses followed by exponential growth. Unchained's traction, Kelly argued, shows the model can work.

"Some of these early LPs are going to start to look for some payouts. Is that going to be a headwind for Bitcoin VC or a confirmation of the model?"

Clark Moody, the only VC on the panel, was more circumspect. Epoch VC runs a sub-$10 million first fund writing seed checks, and Moody said Bitcoin VC as a category is still unproven.

He contrasted Bitcoin-only investing with the broader crypto VC playbook, where funds chased fast liquidity by backing token launches that sidestepped securities laws. Those returns have diminished. "Traditional VC is supposed to be contrarian, but mostly it isn't," Moody said. "Mostly it's just a followers game."

"It's a black hole. It just pulls everything in."

The panel agreed that what Bitcoin founders need from investors extends well beyond capital. Kelly described getting an introduction to a major bank's digital asset team within two days through a lead investor. Hamilton said the in-person relationships concentrated in Nashville, anchored by Bitcoin Park, have been a business accelerant.

He moved from Raleigh to Nashville after visiting six times in one year. The gravity well of builders, investors, and customers in one place proved irresistible.

"Open protocol, no token. And that's why it succeeded. You can't force it where the demand doesn't exist."

Moody threw in a counterpoint to the entire VC thesis. Nostr, he noted, delivered everything the Web3 social companies promised, without a token and without venture funding.

For aspiring founders, Hamilton urged building working prototypes before fundraising, calling AI tools a force multiplier for getting proof of concepts live quickly. Kelly advised questioning assumptions about what problems are already solved. Unchained launched with lending, assuming custody was figured out, then discovered customers still needed the collaborative custody model they had built internally.

The founders on stage have built real businesses serving real customers. Whether that translates into the kind of returns that keep Bitcoin VC alive is the open question hanging over all of them.
