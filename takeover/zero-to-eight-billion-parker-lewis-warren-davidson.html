In this Bitcoin Takeover fireside chat, Parker Lewis and Rep. Warren Davidson discuss the critical role of sound money in defending freedom, the history of monetary policy, and the importance of Bitcoin and free market principles in shaping America's financial future.

Rep. Warren Davidson: Auditing the Fed Is Not a Plan

The Federal Reserve now pays banks a guaranteed 4% on unlimited reserves. Rep. Warren Davidson thinks that explains why common-sense monetary reform keeps dying in Washington.

The Federal Reserve now pays banks a 4% rake on unlimited reserves, guaranteed, risk-free. Rep. Warren Davidson (R-OH) thinks that explains a lot about why common-sense monetary reform keeps dying in Washington. "They have a lot of extra money to lobby to get their way," Davidson said in a fireside chat with Parker Lewis during Bitcoin Takeover at Bitcoin Park. "And they've basically killed common sense policy since at least 2018."

Davidson has been trying to fix that from inside Congress for a decade. He introduced the first Bitcoin bill in Congress in 2018, the Token Taxonomy Act. He has spent 10 years trying to pass a privacy bill and been outlobbied every time. He now carries legislation to ban a central bank digital currency, protect self-custody, and establish a pathway for Americans to pay taxes in Bitcoin and route those payments directly into a strategic Bitcoin reserve.

His diagnosis of the problem runs deeper than any single bill. When Nixon ended Bretton Woods in 1971, he argued, the United States lost the last constraint on government spending. What followed was the Federal Reserve's balance sheet growing from a few hundred million dollars to over $2 trillion during the Bush years, housing inflation, the 2008 financial crisis, and then COVID-era money printing that put inflation back on every grocery receipt. "The $5 footlong is like $12 now," Davidson said. "That's how bad your money's been destroyed."

The core thesis he and Lewis share: scarcity is the foundation of sound money, and sound money is the foundation of limited government. Remove scarcity and you remove the restraint. "An unrestrained government inherently infringes freedom," Davidson said.

Lewis pressed him on a question that frustrates many in the Bitcoin world — why don't more fiscally conservative members of Congress lean into Bitcoin as the solution? Davidson was candid. Some have been orange-pilled and stayed. He described a small-group session where Pierre Rochard and Michael Goldstein helped him walk Thomas Massie and Byron Donalds through Bitcoin hands-on, downloading to a Coldcard, learning to run a node. Four or five members stayed for the full session. Others drifted. The pull of the status quo, and the money defending it, is strong.

He also pushed back on a common Bitcoiner instinct. Rand Paul's "audit the Fed" campaign is popular in liberty circles but, Davidson argued, amounts to theater. "We all know they print money. They tell us they print money," he said. "Auditing the Fed is not a plan."

The actual plan, in his telling, is legislative: pass the Bitcoin for America Act so tax payments in Bitcoin flow into the strategic reserve and break the capital-gains loop; block a CBDC before it becomes entrenched; get the Blockchain Regulatory Certainty Act through to protect developers; and build a political coalition that doesn't depend on one or two major donors.

He compared it to the ants in A Bug's Life. Individual contributions of $50 or $500 matter less than the multiplication effect when everyone gives, votes, and recruits. "If you get everybody participating, it is far more powerful," he said.

Davidson acknowledged the math is still unfavorable. The Bitcoin community is small relative to the lobbying machines defending the existing financial system. But he thinks the playbook scales.
