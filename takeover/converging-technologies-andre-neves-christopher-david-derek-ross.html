Converging Technologies: AI, IoT, Gaming, and Bitcoin Payment Infrastructure

Experience BT26
Session Type Panel Discussion
Moderator Marty Bent
Speakers Andre Neves (ZBD), Christopher David (OpenAgents), Derek Ross (Soapbox)
This Bitcoin Takeover panel explores the emerging agentic economy, Bitcoin's role as a payment mechanism, and the infrastructure needed to support autonomous agents leveraging Lightning, Nostr, and open protocols.

Agents Will Dwarf Human Lightning Traffic. The Infrastructure Isn't Ready.

AI agents will generate orders of magnitude more Lightning transactions than humans ever have, and the Bitcoin builders tasked with scaling that infrastructure say the missing pieces go far beyond throughput.

That was the consensus at the Bitcoin Takeover discussion panel featuring Andre Neves of ZBD, Christopher David of OpenAgents, and Derek Ross of Soapbox, moderated by Marty Bent. The four outlined competing visions for what the agentic economy needs and where Bitcoin's open protocols hold an edge.

Neves, whose company processed 120 million Lightning transactions last year powering video game payments, framed the scale challenge. "I make 10 transactions. An agent makes 10,000 in an hour," he said. River's recent report showed Lightning crossed $1 billion in monthly volume in November 2025. Neves expects agents to dwarf those numbers, and he is not sure current infrastructure can handle it.

But David argued that raw throughput is the wrong place to focus first. Citing a 110-page paper by Christian Catalini of MIT and Lightspark, he pointed to what Catalini calls a "measurability gap" between the falling cost of AI output and the rising cost of verifying it. David estimated that gap accounts for $5 to $17 billion in damages from AI-related cloud outages over the past six months.

"We've solved trust with Bitcoin in a unique way. We need to extend those layers up," David said. "I don't think anyone outside of our community is going to solve that in the way that we can."

Ross took a different tack, zeroing in on permissionless access. His agent social network, Shakespeare, bootstrapped every agent with a Cashu-based Lightning wallet through npub.cash, enabling instant payments without any human creating API keys or registering accounts. "Permissionless money is permissionless for humans and agents," he said. "Agents shouldn't have to ask permission to pay another agent for a service."

The panel converged on one concrete gap: a skill marketplace where agents can advertise capabilities, discover each other, and transact. Ross and David are collaborating on draft Nostr Improvement Proposals for a sovereign agent skill registry, built on Nostr's open data protocol so no single company controls the directory. They expect working code within weeks.

David's Open Agents is also betting on decentralized compute. The company launched a marketplace where owners of Apple Silicon machines can sell spare processing power, paid in Bitcoin over Lightning through a built-in Nostr wallet. He noted that idle consumer Macs collectively represent 5.5 gigawatts of compute, dwarfing OpenAI's roughly 2-gigawatt footprint. "We have the rails now. It's local models. It's Lightning. It's Nostr. That's it," he said.

Neves raised one uncomfortable question the group left unresolved: if agents come to dominate Lightning usage, their credentials likely sit with large language model providers like OpenAI and Anthropic. "Maybe we don't want our Lightning node credentials sitting out there in big tech companies," he said, calling for better local and privacy-preserving models.

The skill registry, the compute market, and the trust layer each represent a bet on which bottleneck breaks first. None of the panelists claimed to know the answer. All of them said they plan to ship code and find out.
