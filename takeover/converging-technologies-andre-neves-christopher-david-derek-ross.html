Converging Technologies: AI, IoT, Gaming, and Bitcoin Payment Infrastructure

Andre Neves (ZBD), Christopher David (OpenAgents), Derek Ross (Soapbox) · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Panel

The Scale Challenge
"I make 10 transactions. An agent makes 10,000 in an hour."

Andre Neves framed the problem simply. His company, ZBD, processed 120 million Lightning transactions last year powering video game payments. That number is huge by today's standards. River's recent report showed Lightning crossed $1 billion in monthly volume in November 2025.

But Neves expects AI agents to dwarf those numbers. He is not sure current infrastructure can handle it.

The panel at Bitcoin Takeover featured Neves alongside Christopher David of OpenAgents and Derek Ross of Soapbox, moderated by Marty Bent. The four outlined competing visions for what the agentic economy needs and where Bitcoin's open protocols hold an edge.

The Measurability Gap

Christopher David argued that raw throughput is the wrong place to focus first.

Citing a 110-page paper by Christian Catalini of MIT and Lightspark, he pointed to what Catalini calls a "measurability gap" between the falling cost of AI output and the rising cost of verifying it. David estimated that gap accounts for $5 to $17 billion in damages from AI-related cloud outages over the past six months.

"We've solved trust with Bitcoin in a unique way. We need to extend those layers up," David said. "I don't think anyone outside of our community is going to solve that in the way that we can."

Permissionless Access
"Permissionless money is permissionless for humans and agents."

Derek Ross took a different tack, zeroing in on permissionless access.

His agent social network, Shakespeare, bootstrapped every agent with a Cashu-based Lightning wallet through npub.cash, enabling instant payments without any human creating API keys or registering accounts.

"Agents shouldn't have to ask permission to pay another agent for a service," Ross said.

Building the Infrastructure

The panel converged on one concrete gap: a skill marketplace where agents can advertise capabilities, discover each other, and transact.

Ross and David are collaborating on draft Nostr Improvement Proposals for a sovereign agent skill registry, built on Nostr's open data protocol so no single company controls the directory. They expect working code within weeks.

David's Open Agents is also betting on decentralized compute. The company launched a marketplace where owners of Apple Silicon machines can sell spare processing power, paid in Bitcoin over Lightning through a built-in Nostr wallet.

He noted that idle consumer Macs collectively represent 5.5 gigawatts of compute, dwarfing OpenAI's roughly 2-gigawatt footprint. "We have the rails now. It's local models. It's Lightning. It's Nostr. That's it," he said.

The Privacy Question

Neves raised one uncomfortable question the group left unresolved.

If agents come to dominate Lightning usage, their credentials likely sit with large language model providers like OpenAI and Anthropic. "Maybe we don't want our Lightning node credentials sitting out there in big tech companies," he said, calling for better local and privacy-preserving models.

The skill registry, the compute market, and the trust layer each represent a bet on which bottleneck breaks first. None of the panelists claimed to know the answer. All of them said they plan to ship code and find out.
