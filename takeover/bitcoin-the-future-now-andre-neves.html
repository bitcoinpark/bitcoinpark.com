"Bitcoin is not inevitable. Bitcoin is a choice made by builders every single day."

Andre Neves (ZBD) · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Keynote

Andre Neves opened his talk with a number: 473. That's how many times journalists, economists, and heads of state have declared Bitcoin dead.

Not one of those obituaries named the people who kept it alive.

Neves is the CEO of ZBD, a company building embedded payments infrastructure for the video gaming industry on top of the Lightning Network. He has been building with Lightning since the protocol launched in 2018, when it was still labeled "reckless" in node software.

"Gamers have usernames and gamer tags. They do not have invoices or QR codes."

The problem Neves kept hitting was friction. Lightning is an interactive protocol. To receive a payment, you had to be online, generate an invoice QR code, send it to the payer, and hope nothing timed out. For gaming — tipping streamers, rewarding players — that loop broke constantly.

In August 2021, Neves released the Lightning Address protocol, which works like email but for Bitcoin. No QR codes, no invoices. Just send to an address. He built it for gamers.

Twenty-seven days later, El Salvador's Bitcoin law went into effect, and millions of people downloaded Lightning wallets for the first time. Many of those wallets already supported Lightning Address because the industry had adopted it in less than a month.

"What she actually learned was proof. Proof that Bitcoin works, proof that it's actually easy to use, and proof that it is for everyone everywhere in the world."

Neves told the story of Maria, a fruit stand owner in El Zonte, El Salvador. A Bitcoiner named Dread helped onboard her into a Lightning wallet and posted her Lightning Address on Twitter.

People in Tokyo, Berlin, Sao Paulo, and Mumbai — strangers Maria would never meet — sent her money in real time, for free.

Today, Lightning Address runs on everything from Cash App to home Lightning nodes.

"Chaincode built a room for me. Vincum built a room for Brazil. And Brazilian builders are building rooms of their own. That's the chain."

In 2018, Neves attended Chaincode Labs' first Lightning Network residency in New York — 10 engineers, two weeks, heads down. No tuition, no equity taken. That room changed his life. It also raised a question: what about the developers who never get in?

Neves is originally from Brazil. When he looked at the Bitcoin developer scene there, he found nothing — no dev meetups, no conferences, no training. He scheduled the first Brazilian dev cohort for March 12, 2020. The pandemic shut it down. They moved everything online, including dev meetups in VR chat rendered at 15 frames per second.

In early 2022, Neves raised philanthropic capital and launched Vincum, a nonprofit focused on Bitcoin development in Brazil. It now runs Bit Devs meetups countrywide, technical conferences, hackathons, and places trained engineers into industry jobs.

"You don't need permission, and you never did."

Neves closed with the current state of Lightning: over a billion dollars in monthly volume, 99 percent payment success rates, subsecond settlement, and 45,000 nodes.

Eight years ago, the software warned you the network was reckless. Now open-source AI tools let a solo developer prototype in a weekend what used to take a team a quarter to build. The barriers to entry have collapsed.

The only thing left is the decision to start.
