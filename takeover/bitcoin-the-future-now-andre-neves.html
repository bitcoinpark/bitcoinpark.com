Bitcoin: The Future Now

Experience BT26
Session Type Presentation
Speaker Andre Neves (ZBD)
This Bitcoin Takeover presentation explores the evolution of Bitcoin and the Lightning Network, highlighting the importance of builders, global inclusivity, and innovative protocols like Lightning Address. Bitcoin's success is a result of deliberate effort and community resilience — and the next builders are already here.

473 Obituaries and Not One Mentioned the Builders. Andre Neves Wants to Change That.

Bitcoin is not inevitable. It's a choice made by builders every single day — and Andre Neves, CEO of ZBD, has been making that choice since Lightning was still labeled "reckless."

Andre Neves opened his Bitcoin Park talk with a number: 473. That's how many times journalists, economists, and heads of state have declared Bitcoin dead. Not one of those obituaries named the people who kept it alive. "Bitcoin is not inevitable," Neves said. "Bitcoin is a choice made by builders every single day."

Neves is the CEO of ZBD, a company building embedded payments infrastructure for the video gaming industry on top of the Lightning Network. He has been building with Lightning since the protocol launched in 2018, when it was still labeled "reckless" in node software. The problem he kept hitting was friction. Lightning is an interactive protocol. To receive a payment, you had to be online, generate an invoice QR code, send it to the payer, and hope nothing timed out. For gaming — tipping streamers, rewarding players — that loop broke constantly.

"Gamers have usernames and gamer tags," Neves said. "They do not have invoices or QR codes."

In August 2021, Neves released the Lightning Address protocol, which works like email but for Bitcoin. No QR codes, no invoices. Just send to an address. He built it for gamers. Twenty-seven days later, El Salvador's Bitcoin law went into effect, and millions of people downloaded Lightning wallets for the first time. Many of those wallets already supported Lightning Address because the industry had adopted it in less than a month.

Neves told the story of Maria, a fruit stand owner in El Zonte, El Salvador. A Bitcoiner named Dread helped onboard her into a Lightning wallet and posted her Lightning Address on Twitter. People in Tokyo, Berlin, Sao Paulo, and Mumbai — strangers Maria would never meet — sent her money in real time, for free. "What she actually learned was proof," Neves said. "Proof that Bitcoin works, proof that it's actually easy to use, and proof that it is for everyone everywhere in the world." Today, Lightning Address runs on everything from Cash App to home Lightning nodes.

But tooling was only part of Neves's story. In 2018, he attended Chaincode Labs' first Lightning Network residency in New York — 10 engineers, two weeks, heads down. No tuition, no equity taken. "They just gathered a bunch of serious developers in the room and said, 'Go deep,'" he said. That room changed his life. It also raised a question: what about the developers who never get in?

Neves is originally from Brazil. When he looked at the Bitcoin developer scene there, he found nothing — no dev meetups, no conferences, no training. He scheduled the first Brazilian dev cohort for March 12, 2020. The pandemic shut it down. They moved everything online, including dev meetups in VR chat rendered at 15 frames per second. In early 2022, Neves raised philanthropic capital and launched Vincum, a nonprofit focused on Bitcoin development in Brazil. It now runs Bit Devs meetups countrywide, technical conferences, hackathons, and places trained engineers into industry jobs.

"Chaincode built a room for me. Vincum built a room for Brazil," Neves said. "And Brazilian builders are building rooms of their own. That's the chain."

He closed with the current state of Lightning: over a billion dollars in monthly volume, 99% payment success rates, subsecond settlement, and 45,000 nodes. Eight years ago, the software warned you the network was reckless. Now open-source AI tools let a solo developer prototype in a weekend what used to take a team a quarter to build. The barriers to entry have collapsed. The only thing left is the decision to start. "You don't need permission," Neves said, "and you never did."
