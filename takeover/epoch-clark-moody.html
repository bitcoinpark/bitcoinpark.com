Clark Moody (Epoch) · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Keynote

The Ghost of Bitcoin Past
"Litecoin, Namecoin, Hobo Nickels. They're all going to replace Bitcoin — faster block times, more throughput, premines, scams. It was all there."

Clark Moody didn't deliver a keynote at Bitcoin Takeover 2026. He delivered a séance.

Framing his talk as a visit from three spirits — Bitcoin past, present, and future — Moody, technical partner at Epoch and creator of the Clark Moody Dashboard, used A Christmas Carol to make one argument: the threats facing Bitcoin are not new, the community keeps repeating the same mistakes, and the time to act is now.

The ghost of Bitcoin past tracked Moody's own career. Building RTBTC in 2011 on Mt. Gox market data before the WebSocket protocol was finished. Cycling through Blockchain and Bitcoin.com during the pre-fork era. Watching the first altcoin wave arrive and collapse.

The cycles, he argued, are eternal. Nothing about the current moment is without precedent.

The Ghost of Bitcoin Present
"People are selling Bitcoin because of quantum computers. Like, what?"

The ghost of Bitcoin present arrived with a specific list: quantum FUD threatening elliptic curve cryptography, FATF putting developers in jail, financial surveillance tightening, and institutional yield products pulling in capital with 11% returns on wrapped BTC exposure.

Moody wasn't dismissive. The issue, he said, isn't that the threats are imaginary. It's that panic and narrative-chasing are a choice.

Quantum computers aren't breaking Bitcoin tomorrow. Developers aren't being rounded up en masse. But the fear is real, and fear drives bad decisions.

The Ghost of Bitcoin Future

The third spirit pointed at unresolved questions: Will covenants come to Bitcoin? Is ecash the answer or a custodial dead end? Will wealth taxes force a jurisdictional exodus?

Moody didn't pretend to have answers. He refused to let the uncertainty become an excuse.

The future is unknowable. The direction is not.

The Time to Act
"Use your own product. Claude can't use your product. Claude doesn't care."

Moody's call to action was concrete. Build privacy tools. Make privacy the default. He asked the room how many wallet operators had implemented Payjoin, then how many hadn't. The room answered.

"Use your own product," he told product managers. "Claude can't use your product. Claude doesn't care. It passed the tests and renders the UI properly. That's Claude using your product."

Build on open protocols — Nostr, Bitcoin — because AI agents can't sign up for Twitter or Signal, but they can use open systems. Fund open source developers. Fund wallets, which he called "the weak link in all of this."

He closed on story. "Tell better stories," he said. "People want to hear stories. People respond to stories."

For Moody, who has watched four distinct cycles of hype, collapse, and rebuild since 2011, that's not a soft closing thought. It's the mechanism. The protocols exist. The tools exist. What's missing is the narrative that makes people pick them up.
