De Minimis, Developer Freedom, and the Future of Bitcoin Payments

David Zell (Bitcoin Policy Institute), Janessa Lopez (Block) · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Panel

8 Billion Forms
"This has moved from being a hypothetical reality to a realistic one."

Janessa Lopez, head of digital assets policy at Block, laid out the scale of the problem. Block has enabled more than 4 million U.S. merchants to accept Bitcoin at the point of sale through Square. Customers can tap their phone to pay, just like any other transaction.

But every one of those purchases triggers a capital gains reporting obligation to the IRS, no matter how small.

The IRS estimates it will receive more than 8 billion broker reporting forms in the first year of crypto tax enforcement. Block expects that number to climb sharply as Bitcoin payment adoption grows, flooding the agency with forms over transactions that generate negligible tax revenue.

The $200 Compromise

A de minimis exemption would set a dollar threshold below which Bitcoin purchases are exempt from capital gains reporting. Block initially proposed $600 per transaction. Skeptics called that too high.

The conversation has since settled around $200, matching the existing exemption for foreign currency payments.

Stablecoins Aren't Bitcoin

But a bill introduced in the House applied the exemption only to stablecoins, which are pegged to the dollar and produce no taxable gain or loss by design.

Lopez argued stablecoins should simply be treated as cash equivalents, with no capital gains reporting at all. The exemption, she said, is needed for assets like Bitcoin that actually fluctuate in value.

"We desperately need this change," she said.

The Trojan Horse Myth

Critics, including Americans for Tax Fairness, have called a crypto de minimis a "Trojan horse for tax evasion." Lopez dismissed the concern. A millionaire looking to dodge taxes, she argued, would use existing loopholes rather than structure hundreds of sub-$200 transactions.

She also pointed to Bank Secrecy Act compliance controls already in place at brokers like Cash App, which flag suspicious patterns automatically.

Block has proposed limiting the exemption to payment activity rather than investment trades, adding anti-structuring rules, and restricting eligibility to digital assets with market capitalizations above $25 billion. That would effectively narrow the field to large, established networks while keeping the policy technology-neutral, an approach Congress prefers to naming Bitcoin in statute.

The Reconciliation Clock

The legislative window is tight. A standalone tax bill has almost no chance of passing. The most likely vehicle is a reconciliation package, and Lopez said the push needs to happen before Congress leaves for August recess and shifts focus to midterm elections.

For Bitcoiners who want to help, Block built btcismoney.xyz, a site with pre-written emails users can send to their representatives.

David Zell, who leads a free legal counsel program at the Bitcoin Policy Institute for Bitcoin builders, urged the audience to contact their offices directly. Whether the exemption lands at $200 or some other number matters less than whether Bitcoin is included at all.

The reconciliation clock is ticking.
