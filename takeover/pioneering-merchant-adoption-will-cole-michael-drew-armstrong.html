Pioneering Merchant Adoption from Point-of-Sale to Business Treasury

Experience BT26
Session Type Panel Discussion
Speakers Will Cole (Zaprite), Michael Atwood (Oshi), Drew Armstrong (Pubkey NYC)
This Bitcoin Takeover panel explores the current state and future potential of Bitcoin adoption among merchants, focusing on Lightning payments, rewards programs, and overcoming technical and cultural barriers.

Bitcoin Merchant Adoption Has Three Bottlenecks. We Can't Agree Which One Is Worst.

Twelve years after Apple Pay launched, roughly 60% of payments still happen by card swipe or tap — not phone. Three Bitcoin practitioners took that statistic as their starting point.

Twelve years after Apple Pay launched, roughly 60% of payments still happen by card swipe or tap — not phone. That statistic framed the mood at the Bitcoin Takeover merchant adoption panel, where three practitioners offered competing diagnoses of what is slowing Bitcoin down at the point of sale.

Will Cole of Zaprite, Michael of Oshi, and Drew Armstrong of Cipher Mining and Pubkey NYC covered merchants accepting Bitcoin, consumers spending it, and the structural problems blocking both. They agreed on the destination. They differed on which obstacle deserves the most urgency.

Cole laid out the closed-ecosystem problem first. Toast dominates the restaurant industry, Clover runs in many locally owned businesses, and Square has its own turf. Each is a sealed stack with no open API for payments integrations. A restaurant using Toast cannot add a Zaprite Bitcoin checkout without replacing its entire POS system. That is a hard ask when the merchant in question does 95% of transactions in dollars and chose Toast for reasons that have nothing to do with currency. "You have very specialized payment rails for different types of businesses," Cole said.

He drew a parallel to eBay and PayPal in the late 1990s. People were reluctant to type credit card numbers online until PayPal created a trusted pool of spendable digital dollars. eBay and PayPal converged because the supply of buyers and sellers aligned. Bitcoin needs the same moment: enough consumer wallets ready to spend, enough merchants ready to receive, to make the connection worth building.

Michael, who has been onboarding merchants since 2020 and now runs Bitcoin rewards and loyalty programs through Oshi, flagged a subtler problem on the consumer side. Even dedicated Bitcoiners hold their coins in multiple contexts — multisig vaults, custodial exchange wallets, phone-based Lightning wallets — and moving between them is non-trivial. "Just because someone has Bitcoin in multisig doesn't mean they're in a position to operationalize it and spend it with a merchant," Cole added.

Armstrong offered an example from Pubkey, the Bitcoin-themed bar in New York City he co-founded. The setup required staff to manually tag every Bitcoin transaction as "BTC" in Square and then direct the customer to a separate iPad running Zeus for the Lightning QR code. It was two extra steps. On a busy Friday night, that was enough to make every bartender anxious. The friction was psychological, not technical, but it was real.

When asked to name the single biggest bottleneck, Cole chose politics. The de minimis tax exemption is essential, he said, and the mechanism matters — it will only move through reconciliation, not standalone legislation. As evidence that small vocal pressure works, he pointed to a $4 trillion spending bill held up for four days under the Biden administration solely over language about self-hosted wallet reporting. "A few people yelling about it" created the delay.

Michael chose culture and behavior. Build the technical infrastructure, fix the tax treatment, and you still have decades of habit change ahead. Apple Pay is the benchmark: universally available, easy to use, and still accounts for less than half of transactions. "Human behavior is a real thing to change," he said.
