"If you're unique in connecting sources and destinations, you're going to have a disproportionate advantage."

Jesse Shrader (Amboss), Yash Belavadi (Surge) · Moderated by Pierre Rochard · Bitcoin Takeover 2026 · Panel

Jesse Shrader described Lightning yield as a toll road business. Nodes that sit at high-traffic intersections in the payment graph earn routing fees from every transaction that passes through them.

Amboss spent five years building machine learning models to help liquidity providers identify where to deploy capital for maximum fee capture. Graph neural networks borrowed from ISP bandwidth planning and pipe flow optimization.

The results have been modest but self-custodied. Last month, Amboss hit 1.5% APY.

"There's no shortage of Bitcoin looking for self-custody yield."

Shrader's waitlist hit 3,700 Bitcoin. A competitor, Stroom, which offered tokens in exchange for people depositing Bitcoin on the Lightning Network under a fully custodial model, could not generate comparable yield and recently shut down.

His point was implicit: custody matters, and the economics only work if the underlying approach is sound.

Running a Lightning node is like raising a Tamagotchi.

Lightning routing is not passive income. Yields are not uniform. They depend on connectivity, uptime, fee management, and whether your node sits on a route anyone actually uses.

Amboss is building managed services to abstract that complexity, but the underlying skill requirement remains.

"I'm sure this room is not comfortable giving up Bitcoin custody to some random token on some EVM chain."

Yash Belavadi came at the problem from DeFi. The existing Bitcoin-backed credit market on Ethereum-based protocols totals roughly $15 billion in wrapped Bitcoin, earning lenders 3 to 4 percent. But utilization is only 20 to 30 percent, and borrowers have to hand their Bitcoin to a custodian on an EVM chain.

Surge, launching in private beta in April at surge.credit, is trying to bring that rate to native Bitcoin. Borrowers put up Bitcoin as collateral without it leaving a Bitcoin address, verified on-chain. Lenders receive stablecoins. The protocol targets 7 percent at launch.

The coordination layer uses multi-party computation rather than a human intermediary, removing the bank-like overhead that makes most Bitcoin lending expensive.

"Lightning is a full reserve system with no credit and no debt creation, and that constraint is the feature."

The oracle problem is the unresolved tension. Automated liquidation requires a price feed, and a single-source oracle can be gamed or manipulated. A recent Binance incident illustrated this with a billion-dollar liquidation cascade. Belavadi acknowledged the risk, arguing that multi-source price aggregation and improved MPC infrastructure have made the technology more reliable since 2023.

Both founders converged on the same premise: everything being built on top of Lightning is an attempt to unlock yield from that constraint without compromising it.
