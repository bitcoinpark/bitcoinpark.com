A Cost of Capital on Lightning and the Birth of Bitcoin-Native Yield

Experience BT26
Session Type Panel Discussion
Moderator Pierre Rochard (The Bitcoin Bond Company)
Speakers Jesse Shrader (Amboss) & Yash Belavadi (Surge)
This Bitcoin Takeover session explores the innovative world of Bitcoin Lightning yield, self-custody, and decentralized finance. Jesse Shrader and Yash Belavadi discuss how the Lightning Network operates, the sources of yield, and the future of Bitcoin-native credit markets.

Bitcoin-Native Yield Is Real, but it Requires Skill

Jesse Shrader built machine learning models to chase routing fees. Yash Belavadi is bringing credit markets to native Bitcoin. Both agree Lightning's constraint is the feature.

The question "where does the yield come from?" sounds simple. At the Bitcoin Takeover panel on Lightning yield and Bitcoin-native credit markets, two founders gave very different answers.

Jesse Shrader, CEO of Amboss, and Yash Belavadi, co-founder of Surge, joined moderator Pierre Rochard to lay out the diverging approaches. Shrader's answer was routing fees. Belavadi's was credit markets. The difference matters because they carry different custody trade-offs, different risk profiles, and very different technical infrastructure.

Shrader described Lightning yield as a toll road business. Nodes that sit at high-traffic intersections in the payment graph earn routing fees from every transaction that passes through them. "If you're unique in connecting sources and destinations, you're going to have a disproportionate advantage," he said. Amboss spent five years building machine learning models — graph neural networks borrowed from ISP bandwidth planning and pipe flow optimization — to help liquidity providers identify where to deploy capital for maximum fee capture.

The results have been modest but self-custodied. Last month, Amboss hit 1.5% APY. A competitor, Stroom, which offered tokens in exchange for people depositing Bitcoin on the Lightning Network under a fully custodial model, could not generate comparable yield and recently shut down. Shrader's point was implicit: custody matters, and the economics only work if the underlying approach is sound. "There's no shortage of Bitcoin looking for self-custody yield," he said. His waitlist hit 3,700 Bitcoin.

But Lightning routing is not passive income. Shrader compared running a node to raising a Tamagotchi. Yields are not uniform. They depend on connectivity, uptime, fee management, and whether your node sits on a route anyone actually uses. Amboss is building managed services to abstract that complexity, but the underlying skill requirement remains.

Belavadi came at the problem from DeFi. The existing Bitcoin-backed credit market on Ethereum-based protocols totals roughly $15 billion in wrapped Bitcoin, earning lenders 3 to 4% — but utilization is only 20 to 30%, and borrowers have to hand their Bitcoin to a custodian on an EVM chain. "I'm sure this room is not comfortable giving up Bitcoin custody to some random token on some EVM chain," he said.

Surge, launching in private beta in April at surge.credit, is trying to bring that rate to native Bitcoin. Borrowers put up Bitcoin as collateral without it leaving a Bitcoin address, verified on-chain. Lenders receive stablecoins. The protocol targets 7% at launch. The coordination layer uses multi-party computation rather than a human intermediary, removing the bank-like overhead that makes most Bitcoin lending expensive.

The oracle problem is the unresolved tension. Automated liquidation requires a price feed, and a single-source oracle can be gamed or manipulated — as a recent Binance incident illustrated with a billion-dollar liquidation cascade. Belavadi acknowledged the risk, arguing that multi-source price aggregation and improved MPC infrastructure have made the technology more reliable since 2023.

Both founders converged on the same premise: Lightning is a full reserve system with no credit and no debt creation, and that constraint is the feature. Everything being built on top of it is an attempt to unlock yield from that constraint without compromising it.
