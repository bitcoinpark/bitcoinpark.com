Park Forum: The Case for Bitcoin in Healthcare

The US Healthcare Mess, and How Bitcoin Revitalizes the Industry

Opening Keynote by Stuart Lackey • April 8, 2026

Thank you to our sponsors: Unchained, Ten31, and Sound HSA

An $83 Implant Revelation Fueled a Healthcare Mission

For more than two decades, Stu Lackey sold surgical implants into some of the country's most expensive operating rooms, and he was good at it. Then he started doing the math.

The implants were titanium, triangular, plasma-sprayed. Surgeons tapped three of them across a patient's SI joint to treat lower back pain. Each one listed at $5,000, making the procedure a $15,000 line item. Hospitals paid it because their reimbursement contracts were structured to reward exactly that. "Guess how much one of these bad boys cost to machine," Lackey said during his keynote at Bitcoin Park in Austin. The audience guessed wrong. The answer was $83.

That gap, $83 in material cost and $5,000 on the invoice, is not a story about fraud. Lackey is careful to say so. "We helped thousands, hundreds of thousands of patients," he explained. "It's not an indictment on the R&D, it's an indictment on the system." What it is, he argues, is a precise illustration of how American healthcare became a $5.3 trillion annual expenditure consuming 18% of GDP, while producing outcomes that rank last among developed nations. Seventy-two percent of U.S. adults are overweight or clinically obese. Three in four carry at least one chronic condition. Every 11 minutes, someone dies by suicide.

The system's architecture, Lackey contends, was broken by design, or at least by accident, from the beginning. It traces back to 1929, when the Baylor Health System in Dallas offered 1,300 schoolteachers 21 hospital visits per year for 50 cents a month. Blue Cross grew from that arrangement. Then World War II wage freezes pushed employers to offer health insurance as a recruitment tool, embedding the third-party payer structure that still covers 160 million Americans today. By 2024, patients pay out of pocket for just 11% of their care, down from 56% in 1960. The intermediary layer now costs $800 billion in administrative overhead alone.

The personal dimension of the crisis landed closer to home. Lackey lost his father to Parkinson's disease, a reminder that no one escapes needing care. But needing care and funding a broken system are different problems. That distinction drove him toward Bitcoin and toward a particular conviction about incentives. LASIK eye surgery has dropped 95% in price over its history because it operates as a cash market. Surgery Center of Oklahoma publishes all prices publicly and has raised them once in 20 years. Direct primary care practices in Nashville run $1,500 to $2,000 annually. The pattern holds: remove the intermediaries, prices fall.

A 2007 meta-analysis by Dr. Steven Schroeder at UCSF, re-examined with identical results in 2024, found that behavior drives 40% of health outcomes, genetics 30%, and healthcare delivery just 10%. Lackey paused on that last number. The country spends five trillion dollars chasing a 10% variable.

His answer is structural. He is launching what he describes as the first Bitcoin health savings account, a self-directed, federally regulated vehicle with triple tax advantages that holds actual Bitcoin rather than cash equivalents. He frames it as sound money meeting sound health: long time preference, transparent pricing, value exchanged directly between patient and provider.

For Lackey, whose career was built inside the system he now critiques, the $83 implant was never just a pricing anomaly. It was the math that stopped mathing, and eventually, the math that changed his mission.
