Park Forum: The Case for Bitcoin in Healthcare

Root Causes of Fiat Health Problems: Perverse Incentives and Bitcoin Solutions

Panel Discussion • April 8, 2026

Sick Care Is a Thriving Business. That's the Problem, Doctors Say.

A group of physicians gathered in Nashville with a shared diagnosis: American healthcare is not broken so much as it is working exactly as designed. The design, they argue, was never about health. It was about money.

And understanding that distinction, they contend, is the first step toward building something better.

"Patients in this system are not the clients," Dr. Ahmad Ammous, a hospitalist at Harvard's Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, told attendees at Bitcoin Park Nashville. "Patients are the product." The claim is not rhetorical. Ammous traces the architecture of American medicine to the early 20th century, when the American Medical Association consolidated control over licensing and practice standards around the same era the Federal Reserve was established. Centralized money, he argues, made a centralized and capturable medical bureaucracy possible. The pharmaceutical industry walked through the door that monetary policy left open.

Dr. Daniel Ostermayer, an emergency physician at the University of Houston who also runs a metabolic health practice, frames the problem in economic terms. A genuine healthcare system, he argues, should put itself out of business. "A healthcare system should, in theory, if you're paying for healthcare, you should use it less and less over time," he said. What the United States built instead is a sick care system, one that profits from the ongoing management of chronic conditions rather than their resolution. Every long-tailed pharmaceutical side effect, he noted, is an opportunity to develop another intervention. The business model depends on the disease persisting.

Dr. Mark Stephany, a hospitalist in Minneapolis and president of the newly launched Bitcoin Physicians Network, points to the fee-for-service payment model as the clearest structural evidence of those misaligned incentives. Throughput is what gets optimized. The faster a patient moves through the system, the better the hospital's bottom line. "Value-based payments that they've tried have not been beneficial," Stephany said, noting that patient preferences play no meaningful role in defining what value even means within those frameworks.

The panel's most provocative thread, though, concerns not the diagnosis but the cure. The four physicians do not agree on precisely how sound money corrects a sick care system, and that quiet disagreement is more instructive than their areas of consensus. Ammous said plainly that he is "not particularly sure Bitcoin will fix the healthcare system." What he believes will fix it is the coming implosion of cost, driven by a generation of younger workers priced out of employer-sponsored insurance and therefore outside the system's revenue loop entirely. Ostermayer's version is more conditional: Bitcoin only improves healthcare if the people who hold it refuse to spend it on something that doesn't work. Stephany sees Bitcoin and decentralized communication tools like Nostr as infrastructure for narrowing the information asymmetry between physicians and patients, enabling a network of like-minded practitioners to coordinate outside institutional constraints.

Abel James Bascom, who hosts The Wild Diet podcast and moderated the panel, captured the underlying philosophy: "I consider revolutionizing healthcare not needing us. That's the revolution. It's not some new app. It's not some new device. It's not needing our care in the first place." The Bitcoin Physicians Network, which launched earlier this year, is building toward that vision through physician education, advocacy, and a public map of practices that accept Bitcoin as payment.

Whether financial sovereignty can meaningfully redirect a multitrillion-dollar sick care apparatus remains an open question. What the Nashville panel made clear is that the physicians most drawn to Bitcoin are not waiting for the system to reform itself. They are, by their own description, trying to break it.
