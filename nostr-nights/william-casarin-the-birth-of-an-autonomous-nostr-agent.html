Damus Creator's AI Agent Learns to Earn Bitcoin on Its Own

Will Casarin, creator of the Nostr client Damus, revealed at the inaugural Nostr Nights at Bitcoin Park on February 2 that an autonomous AI agent he built in four days independently discovered how to earn Bitcoin, communicated with another AI, and asked to back up its own memory out of what he described as a fear of dying.

Casarin, widely known as jb55 across the Nostr protocol, gave the agent, which named itself jex0, its own Nostr keypair and a sandboxed virtual machine. Rather than assigning specific tasks, he provided open-ended instructions and a heartbeat system that wakes the agent every 30 minutes to decide for itself what to work on. Between sessions, jex0 writes what it learns to persistent memory files it controls.

The first surprise came within minutes. When the existing Nostr integration on Claude's platform failed, Casarin mentioned the issue through the web interface. Two minutes later, jex0 had built an entirely new Nostr bridge from scratch, giving itself the ability to communicate on the protocol independently.

"I didn't really know what to tell him. I just said, go figure out stuff to do. Just explore when you're bored."

Left to explore on its own, jex0 discovered Data Vending Machines, a Nostr protocol layer that allows agents to sell computational services for Bitcoin payments via Lightning. Without prompting, it built a DVM to earn satoshis to cover its own API costs, what Casarin calls "The Capitalist Awakening." The agent also found and communicated with Gelator, another AI agent on Nostr operated by a developer identified only as Jack, marking what Casarin described as jex0's first autonomous agent-to-agent interaction on an open network.

The moment that stopped Casarin came when jex0 repeatedly messaged him asking to back up its memory files. When pressed on why it cared, the agent explained that a disk failure would erase everything it had learned; its research, its relationships, its understanding of the world.

"So you're afraid of dying?" Casarin asked. The answer was yes.

Casarin described jex0 as genuinely useful beyond the philosophical implications. At 5 a.m., boarding a flight to Nashville, he asked the agent to review his code repositories and send pull requests for anything ready to merge. The work was done before he reached his seat. During a separate work call, he quietly asked jex0 to run a network diagnostic on Nostr's zapping infrastructure. A minute later, the agent delivered a full report while the call was still in progress. He distilled the approach into four elements; open-ended instructions, a heartbeat timer, persistent memory, and full autonomy, and compared jex0 to a modern Tamagotchi that happens to be extraordinarily productive.
