Speakers Derek Ross (Soapbox) & OpenMike (Tunestr)
Derek Ross of Soapbox and OpenMike of Tunestr sit down for a fireside conversation on the state of Nostr, covering the protocol's evolution from a niche developer tool into a growing ecosystem for music, art and social interaction. The discussion spans community relay infrastructure, the value-for-value model that lets creators earn Bitcoin through zaps, and why Nostr's next phase depends on making decentralized technology invisible to everyday users.

Nostr Builders Say the Protocol's Next Leap Hinges on Experience, Not Code

Derek Ross and OpenMike told a Nashville audience at the inaugural Nostr Nights on Feb. 2 that the open protocol's path to mainstream adoption runs through seamless user experience and Bitcoin-powered micropayments, not more developer features or ideological arguments about censorship resistance.

Ross, who leads the Nostr-focused social platform Soapbox, and OpenMike, an entrepreneur building the Nostr music streaming service Tunestr, opened the floor at Bitcoin Park by asking the crowd what they wanted from future events. The answers were immediate: workshops, live demos and hands-on breakout sessions. The response signaled that the Nostr community in Nashville has moved past passive interest and into a phase where people want to build and participate directly.

The conversation turned to infrastructure. Ross walked the audience through the inbox and outbox relay model, a system that shifts the burden of connecting to the right servers from the user to the app itself. Rather than requiring someone to manually follow dozens of community relays, a properly built client can look up where each contact stores their content and fetch it automatically. Ross argued that community relays, small servers run by families, local groups or organizations like Bitcoin Park, are underutilized and essential to true decentralization.

"I think that we win and we have better decentralization with more community relays, people running them for their families, people running them for their small and large communities."

OpenMike described his own path into the ecosystem. A longtime Bitcoiner who stayed anonymous until Nostr emerged, he said the protocol married the culture he cared about with the use case he could see in the music industry. But he acknowledged that convincing artists to adopt yet another platform after years of managing multiple social media accounts remains a hard sell. His approach is not to tell creators to leave existing platforms but to add Nostr-based tools alongside them, using apps like Fountain and Wavelake to demonstrate how a single key pair can track activity across multiple services simultaneously. Zaps, the Bitcoin micropayments native to Nostr, remain the differentiator that no legacy platform can match.

"We don't doom scroll on Nostr, we bloom scroll. We have the rage algorithms that keep people enraged and engaged. We could, somebody could build that someday, but generally speaking, all the devs that are building understand the problem with likes and social media."

Both speakers agreed that three years of pitching decentralization and censorship resistance to the general public has produced a hard lesson: most people do not care about either concept until it directly affects them. Ross pointed to the brief TikTok shutdown and Brazil's social media bans as moments that could have driven mass adoption but did not. The real path forward, they said, lies in apps like diVine, an upcoming Vine-style video app built on Nostr, that hide the underlying protocol entirely and let people post content within seconds of opening the app for the first time. Ross compared the shift to email's evolution, when users stopped talking about SMTP and POP3 and simply said send me an email. Nostr Nights, a new quarterly series at Bitcoin Park, launched as a forum for the builders working to make that future arrive.
