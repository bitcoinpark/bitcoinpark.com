State of Nostr Fireside

Experience Nostr Nights
Session Type Fireside Chat
Date February 2, 2026
Speakers Derek Ross (Soapbox) & OpenMike (Tunestr)
Derek Ross of Soapbox and OpenMike of Tunestr sit down for a fireside conversation on the state of Nostr, covering the protocol's evolution from a niche developer tool into a growing ecosystem for music, art and social interaction. The discussion spans community relay infrastructure, the value-for-value model that lets creators earn Bitcoin through zaps, and why Nostr's next phase depends on making decentralized technology invisible to everyday users.

How Nostr Builders Learned to Stop Selling and Start Shipping

At the inaugural Nostr Nights, Derek Ross, developer relations at Soapbox, and OpenMike, the entrepreneur behind the Nostr streaming service tunestr.io, described how their failed strategies for onboarding users reshaped their entire approach to bringing people onto the protocol.

Ross, who leads the Nostr-focused social platform Soapbox, arrived at the protocol convinced the pitch was self-evident. Decentralization. Censorship resistance. He repeated those words at meetups and conferences, certain they would resonate. They did not. When the TikTok shutdown rattled users, they moved to BlueSky. When Brazil banned certain social platforms, displaced users waited for the bans to lift. After watching one catalytic moment after another fail, Ross reached an uncomfortable conclusion.

"I realize that nobody knows what those two things mean, and they literally don't care about it."
That admission became the pivot point for his entire approach.

OpenMike's journey ran in a different direction but landed in the same place. An entrepreneur building the Nostr music streaming service Tunestr, he worked in Bitcoin spaces while keeping that identity walled off from his music community. Musicians already juggle six social media accounts, performing algorithmic dances on each one. Telling them to add a seventh was never going to work. The shift came when he recognized that Nostr married his Bitcoin worldview with a practical use case for creators.

"I'm already having a hard time keeping my mouth shut about Bitcoin, but now that these two things are married, I got to get out there."
He stopped pitching Nostr as a platform and started introducing it as tools layered alongside what artists already use.

That reframing changed his onboarding playbook. OpenMike walks musicians through apps like Fountain and Wavelake, ports their key pair; the cryptographic identity that follows a user across Nostr applications; across each one, then zaps a favorite song. Zaps are Bitcoin micropayments native to the protocol, and watching value flow across four independent apps from a single action tends to get attention. But he insists the real engine is peer-to-peer.

"It can't be us telling them. It's got to be musicians telling other musicians, artists telling other artists."
The strategy depends on creators evangelizing to their own communities, not outsiders lecturing about decentralization.

Ross redirected his energy toward infrastructure users never think about. He made the case for community relays; small servers operated by families, local groups, or organizations like Bitcoin Park. The inbox and outbox relay model, where apps automatically locate each contact's stored content without requiring users to manage servers, shifts the burden onto software.

"I think that we win and we have better decentralization with more community relays, people running them for their families, people running them for their small and large communities."
Both speakers pointed to diVine, an upcoming Vine-style video app built on Nostr, as a model. A new user opens the app, agrees to terms, and posts within seconds. No key management. No relay configuration. The protocol stays hidden.

The transformations Ross and OpenMike described mirror a broader reckoning inside the Nostr ecosystem. Builders who once led with ideology now lead with experience design, burying the protocol beneath interfaces that feel familiar. OpenMike, who worked alongside collaborator Jim Costello to crack the music onboarding problem, maintained the long road is worth walking. "You can't unsee it when you navigate the music," he said. Whether invisible infrastructure will succeed where ideological arguments failed remains open, but both left the stage convinced they are no longer making the same mistake twice.
